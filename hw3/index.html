Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection (20 Points)

Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.

First, we convert the field of view values from degrees to radians, and then we map to the camera space coordinates. This is done by subtracting 0.5 from the coordiates to center them around zero, and then multiplying them by two to map them to the range [-1, 1]. Then, they are scaled by the tangent of half of the field of view to finish the mapping. Then, we define the ray direction in the camera space using Z = -1 for the sensor postion, and then map that direction back to the world space using the camera-to-world matrix. This is then normalized so it is a unit vector. Finally, the ray is created in the world space starting from the camera's position ad the near and far clipping planes are set. These clipping planes ensure the tracer won't intersect with geometry outside of the field of view. Next, we need to calculate the final pixel color. The raytrace_pixel function generates random samples at the sub-pixel level, normalizes their coordinates, and then averages radiance from rays shot into the scene to calculate the final color.


Explain the triangle intersection algorithm you implemented in your own words.

For the triangle intersection, first, the edges of the triangle are computed, and then the determinant of these edges is calculated. If this determinant is close to zero, then that means the ray is parallel to the triangle and that there is no intersection. If not zero, then the barycentric coordinates are computed along with the t-value. The t value is then checked to see if it's within the ray bounds. If an intersection occurs, it is recorded in the Intersection data. A similar strategy was used for the sphere intersection. The ray equation is used with the equation for the sphere to check for an intersection, and any that occur are recorded.

Part 4: Global Illumination (20 Points)

Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.

In reality, light bounces off surfaces infinitely until their energy dies out. This is not possible to do with computation, so we need to have a way to decide when the bounces stop to prevent an infinite render time. When a ray bounces off of a surface, some of the light goes straight to the camera. If isAccumBounces is true, this direct light gets added to the total L_out at each step of the ray's path. To give a reasonable cut-off that doesn't result in artificial darkness, we use the coin_flip function to randomly terminate 30% of the rays. At each bounce, 30% of the rays are stopped, and the other 70% keep going. To prevent bias in the final image, the remaining rays that survive get a boost in brightness by dividing their light by the cpdf probability. At each bounce, the new direction of the light needs to be determined. This direction depends on the material. The isect.bsdf->sample_f function takes generates a valid random direction for the ray to travel in, w_in. It also provides how much light is reflected from the ray coming in to the ray coming out based on the material, along with the probability that the selected direction would be chosed. A new ray is then created with this direction and its depth is reduced by one. The function will then call itself to continue the ray's path. Finally, once the ray finishes it's path, we use the Monte Carlo estimate to get the final radiance.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling (20 Points)

Explain adaptive sampling. Walk through your implementation of the adaptive sampling.

Adaptive sampling is a method of sampling during rendering that helps reduce noise. Noise reduction can be achieved by increasing the number of samples in a pixel, but it's not necessary to do for every pixel. Instead of testing every pixel with, say, 1024 rays, we start with a smaller amount (the batch number) of 32. Once that amount of rays has been done, adaptive sampling can look at the difference between the results of those rays. If they're all basically the same (within a 95% confidence interval), we can confidently assume that we have the right color for the pixel and can stop. If those 32 rays have wildly different results, then we know we should continue shooting more rays at it to help smooth out noise. Being able to make this skip once we've determined we have enough info helps speed up rendering while still removing noise where it crops up.
